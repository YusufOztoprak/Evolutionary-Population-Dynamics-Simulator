1. Genetic Trait Representation

Each individual \( i \) is represented by a single numerical genetic trait \( x_i \). This abstraction allows us to model evolution mathematically as an optimization problem in a continuous space.

2. Fitness Function (Gaussian)

We calculate the fitness \( f(x) \) of an individual based on how close their trait \( x \) is to the environmental optimum \( x_{opt} \). We use a Gaussian (Bell Curve) function:

$$ f(x) = e^{-\frac{(x - x_{opt})^2}{2\sigma^2}} $$

Where:

  • \( x_{opt} \): The optimal trait value (Target).
  • \( \sigma \): The tolerance (width of the curve). Smaller \(\sigma\) means stricter selection.
This function ensures that fitness is maximized (1.0) when \( x = x_{opt} \) and decays to 0 as the individual deviates from the optimum.

3. Mutation Model (Stochastic)

When an offspring is produced, its genetic trait is not an exact copy of the parent. We add random Gaussian noise:

$$ x_{new} = x_{parent} + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_m) $$

Where \( \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_m) \) is a random number drawn from a normal distribution with mean 0 and standard deviation \( \sigma_m \) (Mutation Rate).

4. Population Growth (Logistic)

To simulate resource competition, we use the discrete Logistic Growth equation to determine the population size for the next generation:

$$ N_{t+1} = N_t + r N_t \left( 1 - \frac{N_t}{K} \right) $$

Where:

  • \( N_t \): Current population size.
  • \( r \): Growth rate.
  • \( K \): Carrying Capacity (maximum population the environment can support).
The term \( (1 - N_t/K) \) represents environmental resistance. As \( N_t \) approaches \( K \), growth slows down to zero.
